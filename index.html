Cricklytics

Introduction

Cricket, once governed purely by instinct and experience, has evolved into a sport heavily influenced by data-driven decisions. From team selection to in-game strategy, performance analytics has become central to gaining a competitive edge. The rise of global tournaments, fantasy leagues, and high-stakes international fixtures has intensified the demand for intelligent systems that can interpret player performance and predict match outcomes with reliability.

This project, titled Cricklytics, is built around the core idea of transforming raw cricket performance data into actionable insights. It aims to simulate how selectors, analysts, and strategists might approach key decisions if they had access to a fully quantified view of player capabilities.

The primary objectives of this project are twofold:

  • Best XI Selector: Given a set of international teams, the system dynamically constructs an optimal playing XI based on player roles, historical consistency, and all-round performance. It ensures balance across key roles such as openers, middle-order anchors, finishers, all-rounders, and specialist bowlers, emulating the complexity of real-world team composition.
  • Win Probability Estimator: By analyzing the performance strength of individual squads, the model predicts the likelihood of one team defeating another. This feature is designed to simulate pre-match strategy scenarios, aiding analysts, fans, and fantasy players in anticipating likely outcomes based on past performance data.

The design of Cricklytics is inspired by how modern teams evaluate players not just on reputation or individual stats in isolation, but on how their roles, styles, and impact align with broader team dynamics. By objectively scoring each player's contribution across multiple dimensions, Cricklytics delivers a comprehensive and unbiased framework for team analysis and match prediction.

The system will rely on advanced data processing, feature engineering, performance modeling, and ML-driven decision-making. It can serve applications in sports analytics, fantasy leagues, coaching dashboards, and strategic planning for team management.

Ultimately, this system reflects the future of cricket intelligence — where intuition meets data, and selection meets simulation.

Player Role Classification and Selection Criteria

Constructing a balanced and high-performing cricket team requires more than just selecting players with impressive statistics. The best teams are those that strategically distribute roles across batting, bowling, and all-round capabilities while ensuring adaptability to various match conditions. This section outlines the data-driven methodology employed to identify the Best Playing XI, ensuring each selected player fits a well-defined cricketing role based on historical performance metrics.

To simulate real-world team composition logic, the squad was designed to include:

  • • 2 Openers
  • • 3 Middle-Order Anchors
  • • 1 Finisher
  • • 2 All-Rounders
  • • 3 Specialist Fast Bowlers

Each category has distinct expectations, and thus, role-specific selection criteria were developed as follows:

Openers (2 Players)

  • • Batting Average > 30: Reflects consistency and ability to occupy the crease
  • • Strike Rate > 140: Indicates aggressive scoring ability
  • • Boundary Percentage > 50%: Shows intent and ability to dominate bowlers early
  • • Players with an average batting position of 1-2 and a high boundary conversion rate were prioritized.

Middle-Order Batsmen (3 Players)

  • • Batting Average > 40: Ensures reliability under pressure
  • • Strike Rate > 125: Balances aggression with sustainability
  • • Average Balls Faced > 20: Indicates capacity to build an innings and absorb pressure
  • • These players typically bat in positions 3-5 and anchor the innings during middle overs.

Finisher (1 Player)

  • • Batting Average > 25: Demonstrates ability to score consistently
  • • Strike Rate > 130: Emphasizes explosive scoring in short timeframes
  • • Batting Position > 5 and Avg Balls Faced > 15: Indicates lower-order presence with enough impact time
  • • Additionally, finishers who contributed with the ball were marked for bonus consideration.

All-Rounders (2 Players)

  • • Batting Average > 15 and Strike Rate > 140: Indicates useful contributions with the bat
  • • Bowling Economy < 7 and Bowling Strike Rate < 20: Reflects efficiency and wicket-taking ability
  • • Minimum threshold for innings batted and bowled to ensure regular involvement in both roles
  • • Preference was given to players with a measurable impact in both disciplines across multiple matches.

Specialist Fast Bowlers (3 Players)

  • • Bowling Economy < 7: Restricts scoring opportunities
  • • Bowling Strike Rate < 16: Highlights wicket-taking frequency
  • • Dot Ball Percentage > 40%: Indicates control and pressure-building capability
  • • Minimum 6 innings bowled for consistency
  • • Only seamers and pace bowlers were considered under this category to maintain role specificity.

This layered and data-driven selection framework ensures that the final playing XI is not just a lineup of statistically strong individuals, but a strategically balanced squad designed to complement each other's roles — emulating how top-tier teams approach modern cricket selection.

Data Preparation

Effective Data Preparation

Effective data preparation is the foundation for any successful analytics project. In Cricklytics, we worked with scraped JSON data from ESPNcricinfo and transformed it into structured, reliable, and linkable CSV datasets. The entire preprocessing pipeline was broken down into four major components: match summary, batting, bowling, and player metadata. Each was cleaned, standardized, and made analysis-ready.

Data Preprocessing Pipeline

1. Match Summary Processing

Key steps:

  • Loaded raw match summary data from a JSON structure.
  • Renamed scorecard to match_id for consistency.
  • Created a match_ids_dict using team combinations (e.g., "India Vs Australia").
  • Enabled efficient joining of batting and bowling datasets by match.
  • Exported as match Summary.csv.

2. Batting Summary Processing

Key steps:

  • Imported match-wise batting data from JSON.
  • Added out/not_out column from dismissal info.
  • Linked rows with match_id using match_ids_dict.
  • Cleaned names by removing symbols (c), †.
  • Dropped redundant dismissal column.
  • Saved as batting Summary.csv.

3. Bowling Summary Processing

Key steps:

  • Loaded data from JSON.
  • Mapped records to match_id.
  • Ensured numeric typing for overs, wickets, economy.
  • Saved as bowling Summary.csv.

4. Player Information Processing

Key steps:

  • Loaded player metadata from JSON.
  • Filled missing description with "No description available".
  • Replaced null bowlingStyle with "Not Applicable".
  • Removed duplicate and non-informative records.
  • Aligned names by stripping symbols ((c), †).
  • Saved as players Info.csv.

📊 CSV Column Summaries

📄 match Summary.csv

  • team1, team2: Competing teams.
  • winner, margin: Match outcome.
  • ground, matchDate: Venue and date.
  • match_id: Unique join key.

📄 batting Summary.csv

  • batsmanName, teamInnings, battingPos, runs, balls, 4s, 6s, SR
  • out/not_out: Binary dismissal indicator.
  • match_id: Match identifier.

📄 bowling Summary.csv

  • bowlerName, bowlingTeam, overs, maiden, runs, wickets, economy
  • 0s, 4s, 6s, wides, noBalls: Ball-by-ball insights.
  • match_id: Match link key.

📄 players Info.csv

  • name: Player name.
  • battingStyle, bowlingStyle: Technical profiles.
  • playingRole: Role type (e.g., Allrounder, Bowler).
  • description: Player bio or context.

Metric Definition & Design Using DAX Logic

To derive meaningful insights from raw cricket performance data, we developed a curated set of measures and calculated columns. These metrics serve as the foundation for player evaluation, comparison, and modeling. All DAX logic was derived from cricketing principles and mapped directly to the processed tables (batting Summary, bowling Summary).

🏏 Batting Performance Metrics

  • Total Runs: Captures the cumulative run-scoring ability of a player across all innings. High total runs reflect both experience and impact.
  • Total Innings Batted: Indicates how frequently a batsman gets the opportunity to bat, helping normalize other metrics like average or strike rate.
  • Total Innings Dismissed: Shows the number of times a player got out; essential for calculating batting average and understanding reliability.
  • Batting Average: Measures consistency; a high average suggests the player is dependable and contributes consistently.
  • Total Balls Faced: Indicates how long a player typically stays at the crease; important for identifying anchors and innings-builders.
  • Strike Rate: Reflects how quickly a player scores; vital for distinguishing aggressive hitters and traditional players.
  • Batting Position: Represents typical lineup position; helps classify players into openers, middle-order, or lower-order roles.
  • Boundary Percentage: Measures how much of a player's runs come from boundaries; high value indicates aggressive batting.
  • Average Balls Faced per Innings: Gives insight into a player's ability to handle pressure and anchor long innings.

🎯 Bowling Performance Metrics

  • Wickets: Tracks how many dismissals a bowler has achieved; a direct indicator of bowling effectiveness.
  • Balls Bowled: Total deliveries bowled; helps contextualize strike rate, economy, and dot-ball metrics.
  • Runs Conceded: Indicates runs a bowler gave away; crucial for measuring control and efficiency.
  • Bowling Economy: Average runs conceded per over; key in white-ball formats for run control.
  • Bowling Strike Rate: How often a bowler takes wickets; lower values mean higher efficiency.
  • Bowling Average: Runs conceded per wicket; balanced bowlers tend to have a lower average.
  • Total Innings Bowled: Number of matches bowled; indicates experience and workload.
  • Dot Ball Percentage: How often the bowler delivers non-scoring balls; identifies pressure builders and economical bowlers.

🧮 Calculated Columns for Enhanced Insight

  • Boundary Runs (Batting): Derived metric showing runs scored from just boundaries; assesses attacking contribution.
  • Boundary Runs (Bowling): Measures runs conceded through boundaries; assesses vulnerability against aggressive batting.

These measures are integral to every component of the Cricklytics system—from player scoring and Best XI selection to team strength evaluation and match outcome prediction.

Best XI Selector Role-Based Team Optimization

🎯 Objective

The Best XI Selector is designed to automatically generate the most balanced and high-performing cricket team from any given set of countries. It ensures the selected XI covers all critical roles—openers, middle-order batters, finishers, all-rounders, and bowlers—while maximizing overall impact using a custom scoring system.

🧠 Selection Strategy

Players are categorized into functional roles based on historical performance patterns using a performance-driven classification system. Dynamically assigned roles include:

  • Openers (2): Top-order batters with high strike rate, average, and boundary percentage.
  • Middle Order (3): Anchors facing more deliveries with solid averages who accelerate innings.
  • Finisher (1): Impact batters in lower positions with high strike rates finishing games under pressure.
  • All-Rounders (2): Players meeting batting and bowling thresholds, contributing in both disciplines.
  • Fast Bowlers (3): Specialists with economical figures, frequent wickets, and high dot-ball rates.

⚙️ Role Assignment Logic

Metrics like batting average, strike rate, batting position, wickets, economy, and bowling strike rate determine roles. For example:

  • A low-order batter with a high strike rate becomes a Finisher.
  • A bowler with excellent economy and high wicket tally becomes a Fast Bowler.
  • Players excelling in both are classified as All-Rounders.

📊 Scoring System

Each player receives a total score balancing batting and bowling metrics:

  • Batting Score: Batting average, strike rate, boundary percentage.
  • Bowling Score: Wickets, economy, pressure-building capabilities.
  • Total Score: Sum of Batting Score and Bowling Score; top scores within roles are selected.

🔄 Fallback Strategy

If fewer players exist in a role, fallback rules fill gaps:

  • Add extra bowler if only one all-rounder is found.
  • If no all-rounders, fill slots with top-ranked Bowlers, Finishers, or Middle Order players.

This guarantees 11 players while preserving team balance.

Output

The final output is a ranked Best XI list by role type and impact score, supporting:

  • Strategic team planning
  • Fantasy league optimization
  • Comparative player analytics

Match Outcome Predictor - Team Strength-Based Win Probability

🎯 Objective

The Match Outcome Predictor estimates win probability between two international teams by evaluating combined player strength, making it a data-driven, player-centric prediction engine.

⚙️ Methodology

  1. Team Roster Identification: Identify squads from batting and bowling datasets (up to 11 players per team).
  2. Performance Aggregation: Compute each player's Batting Score (average, strike rate, boundary %) and Bowling Score (wickets, economy), combined into a Total Impact Score.
  3. Team Strength Calculation: Aggregate mean batting averages, strike rates, total wickets, economy rates, and total impact scores for each team.

📈 Probability Computation

Calculate win probabilities using normalized score ratios:

  • Win Probability (Team A) = Team A Score / (Team A Score + Team B Score)
  • Win Probability (Team B) = 1 - Team A Probability

Output Format

The final output presents:

  • India Win Probability: 57.58%
  • Pakistan Win Probability: 42.42%

Applications include:

  • Pre-match analysis for broadcasters and strategists
  • Fantasy team boosters
  • Simulation-based tournament modeling

Future Scope

While Cricklytics delivers a strong foundation, there are several promising directions to enhance its capability and impact:

  • Machine Learning-Based Win Prediction: Train supervised models (e.g., logistic regression, XGBoost) using historical match outcomes and aggregated team stats for more nuanced and probabilistic outcome prediction.
  • Real-Time Simulation Engine: Integrate ball-by-ball data to simulate dynamic win probabilities during a live match using Monte Carlo or Bayesian approaches.
  • Role Clustering with Unsupervised Learning: Use clustering algorithms (e.g., K-Means, DBSCAN) to automatically group players into tactical roles based on multidimensional stats—offering data-driven role identification.
  • Player Performance Forecasting: Build time-series models (e.g., ARIMA, Prophet) to forecast future player form and likely performance in upcoming fixtures.
  • Interactive Dashboards & Visualizations: Expand the visual layer using BI tools (Power BI, Tableau) to allow selectors, coaches, and fans to explore trends, matchups, and scenarios in real-time.
  • Integration with Fantasy Cricket APIs: Extend the project into a consumer-facing application by providing fantasy team suggestions, captain/vice-captain picks, and trade optimization based on upcoming matches.

Cricklytics not only solves current analytical needs but also lays the groundwork for scalable cricket intelligence systems. With the right integrations and ML modeling, it has the potential to become a full-fledged analytics suite for selectors, coaches, fans, and broadcasters alike.
